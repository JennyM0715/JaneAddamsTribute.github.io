TIMELINE
1860
On September 6, 1860, Jane Addams was born in Cedarville, Illinois eighth of John H. and Sarah Weber Addams
1881
Graduated from Rockford Female Seminary with a collegiate certificate and membership in Phi Beta Kappa
1886 & 1887
The idea of democracy as a social ideal, questioning the social pressures on a woman to marry and devote her life to family, and the idea of starting a settlement house are influenced by the readings Giuseppe Mazzini's Duties of Man, John Stuart Mill's The Subjection of Women, and traveling to London to see the world's first settlement house, Toynbee Hall
1887
By the end of 1887, Addams discovered her journey of an unending effort, a struggle to reinvigorate her own culture by reconnecting with diversity and conflict of the immigrant communities in America's cities and with the necessities of social reform
1889
Addams and her friend, Ellen Gates Starr, co-founded Hull House in Chicago, Illinois
1894
Addams became the first woman to be appointed as sanitary inspector of Chicago's 19th Ward. The rate of death and disease trasmission was reduced, with the help of the Hull-House Women's Club, reporting over 1,000 health department violations to city counsel and garbage collection
1901
Addams and her collegue founded the Juvenile Protective Association. JPLA engaged in many studies examining subjects such as racism, child labor and exploitation, drug abuse and prostitution in Chicago and their effects on child development
1909
Published The Spirit of Youth and the City Streets, which argued that play and recreation programs are needed because cities are destroying the spirit of youth
1912
Published A New Conscience and an Ancient Evil, which scrutinized the social pathology of sex slavery, prostitution and other sexual behaviors among working class women in American industrial centers during 1890–1910
1912
Addams helped start the new Progressive Party. "Addams' philosophy combined feminist sensibilities with an unwavering commitment to social improvement through cooperative efforts. Although she sympathized with feminists, socialists, and pacifists, Addams refused to be labeled. This refusal was pragmatic rather than ideological."
1919
Founded the Women's International League for Peace and Freedom and was elected president of the International Committee of Women for a Permanent Peace
1931
The Nobel Peace Prize was awarded to Jane Addams "for their assiduous effort to revive the ideal of peace and to rekindle the spirit of peace in their own nation and in the whole of mankind"
May 21, 1935
Jane Addams dies after she was unable to fully recover from a heart attack in 1926
